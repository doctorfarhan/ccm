Introduction to Noradrenaline

üß¨ Pharmacology

Noradrenaline (norepinephrine) is a potent endogenous catecholamine that functions as both a neurotransmitter and hormone. It acts primarily as an Œ±1-adrenergic agonist with some Œ≤1-adrenergic activity.

  • Mechanism: Vasoconstriction via Œ±1-receptors
  • Effects: Increases SVR and MAP
  • Half-life: 2-3 minutes
  • Metabolism: MAO and COMT

‚öïÔ∏è Clinical Indications

Noradrenaline is the first-line vasopressor recommended for septic shock by major guidelines including the Surviving Sepsis Campaign (2021).

  • Septic shock (first-line)
  • Distributive shock
  • Post-operative hypotension
  • Cardiogenic shock (selected cases)

‚úÖ Advantages

  • Lower arrhythmia risk vs dopamine
  • Predictable dose-response
  • Preserves renal blood flow
  • Extensive evidence base
  • Well-established safety profile

  • Requires central venous access
  • Risk of tissue necrosis if extravasated
  • May reduce splanchnic blood flow
  • Contraindicated in uncorrected hypovolemia
  • Monitor for digital ischemia

üìù Clinical Notes & Guidelines

  • Starting Dose: Typically 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min (3.5-7 mcg/min for 70kg patient)
  • Titration: Increase by 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min every 3-5 minutes based on response
  • Maximum Dose: Generally avoid >1 mcg/kg/min; consider additional vasopressors
  • Central Line: Mandatory for concentrations >80 mcg/ml due to extravasation risk
  • Monitoring: Continuous BP, HR, urine output, and peripheral perfusion
  • Weaning: Reduce gradually by 25-50% every 30-60 minutes as tolerated
  • Compatibility: Compatible with D5W, normal saline; avoid alkaline solutions
  • Stability: Use within 24 hours of preparation; protect from light

üî¨ Clinical Evidence for Noradrenaline

SOAP II Trial - Landmark Study Establishing Noradrenaline Superiority
2010

Study: Comparison of Dopamine and Norepinephrine in the Treatment of Shock (De Backer et al., 2010)

Design: Multicenter, randomized, double-blind trial (n=1,679)

Key Findings:

  • No significant difference in 28-day mortality (52.5% vs 48.5%, p=0.10)
  • Significantly lower arrhythmia rate with noradrenaline (12.4% vs 24.1%, p<0.001)
  • Dopamine associated with increased mortality in cardiogenic shock subgroup
  • Established noradrenaline as preferred first-line vasopressor
VASST Trial - Vasopressin vs Noradrenaline
2008

Study: Vasopressin versus Norepinephrine Infusion in Patients with Septic Shock (Russell et al., 2008)

Design: Multicenter, randomized, double-blind trial (n=778)

Key Findings:

  • No difference in 28-day mortality (35.4% vs 39.3%, p=0.26)
  • Vasopressin effective as noradrenaline-sparing agent
  • Trend toward benefit in less severe septic shock
  • Similar safety profile between agents
Early Noradrenaline Administration - 2024-2025 Evidence
2024-2025

Recent Meta-Analysis: Early norepinephrine for patients with septic shock (Shi et al., 2025)

Surviving Sepsis Campaign Guidelines Evolution
2004-2021

Guideline Progression:

  • 2004: First formal sepsis guidelines published
  • 2008: Dopamine and noradrenaline both recommended as first-line
  • 2012: Post-SOAP II shift toward noradrenaline preference
  • 2016: Noradrenaline established as first-line vasopressor
  • 2021: Continued noradrenaline recommendation with emphasis on early initiation
Dosing Evidence Base
Multiple Studies

Starting Dose Recommendations:

  • Typical range: 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min (SSC 2021)
  • Maximum dose: Generally <1 mcg/kg/min before adding second agent
  • Titration: 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min increments every 3-5 minutes

Concentration Evidence:

  • Standard 4mg/250ml (16 mcg/ml) most common
  • Higher concentrations (80-320 mcg/ml) for syringe pumps
  • Central line mandatory for concentrated solutions
